Top Stories
Strait of Hormuz Near Shutdown as Iran War Intensifies
Rigzone / CNBC / Financial Times / Upstream Online
Maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has ground to a near-complete halt, with no oil shipments recorded in the past 24 hours as the U.S.-Israeli Operation Epic Fury against Iran enters its seventh day. Brent crude broke above $90 per barrel for the first time since the conflict began, while energy exporters across the Gulf scramble to secure alternative routing. Trump declared he will accept nothing short of Iran's unconditional surrender, signaling a prolonged conflict with no near-term resolution in sight.
Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG Complex Faces Open-Ended Shutdown
NGI / Financial Times
QatarEnergy CEO Saad Al-Kaabi told the Financial Times that the Ras Laffan Industrial Complex — the world's largest LNG production hub — will not resume operations until the Middle East conflict ends completely, with no timeline given. Qatar has begun leasing idle LNG tankers as the facility remains shuttered, threatening a significant reduction in global LNG supply at a time when alternative sources are already stretched. The shutdown adds acute upward pressure on global gas prices and amplifies supply security concerns for European and Asian importers.
Trump Admin Launches $20B Tanker Reinsurance Program Amid Iran War
CNBC / World Oil
The Trump administration announced a $20 billion reinsurance program for oil tankers as private insurers pull back coverage amid the escalating Iran conflict and the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz. The program is designed to keep critical crude flows moving by backstopping war-risk coverage that commercial markets are increasingly unwilling to provide. The IEA separately said it sees no need yet to release emergency strategic petroleum reserves, though traders are watching both developments closely for signals on supply management.
U.S. Gas Prices Post Biggest Weekly Spike Since Russia-Ukraine War
OilPrice / NGI
The U.S. national average gasoline price recorded its largest weekly jump since the early days of the Russia-Ukraine war as Operation Epic Fury drives crude prices sharply higher, with WTI up roughly 14% on the week. Trump has downplayed the price surge even as consumer sentiment risks deteriorating, with a weak jobs report adding further pressure on the administration's economic narrative. Lower 48 natural gas futures also rallied in tandem with oil, underscoring how the geopolitical shock is pulling typically divergent energy markets higher together.
U.S. electricity generation in 2025 hit a record, again
EIA
U.S. electricity net generation reached a record in 2025 based on data from our Electricity Data Browser. In 2025, the United States generated 4.43 terawatthours (TWh) of electricity, up 2.8% from 2024 generation, which previously had been the highest annual total in our Monthly Energy Review Data Browser dataset dating back to 1949.
Market Outlook
Energy markets are in full geopolitical crisis mode, with WTI at $90.40 and Brent at $92.49 reflecting what is now a dual physical disruption — the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed and Ras Laffan offline indefinitely — a combination not seen in modern market history. The WTI futures curve's steep contango, dropping from $90.81 in Apr 2026 to $74.85 by Aug 2026, suggests the market is treating this as a severe but theoretically time-limited shock, meaning any diplomatic breakthrough or SPR release signal could trigger a violent reversal and traders should manage tail risk accordingly. The IEA's explicit decision to hold SPR releases for now removes a key downside catalyst in the near term, but that posture could flip quickly if Brent pushes toward $100, a level that historically forces coordinated consumer-nation responses. The Trump administration's $20 billion tanker reinsurance program is a critical structural support for physical crude flows — if it successfully keeps alternative-routed tankers moving, it could cap the upside on supply disruption premiums, but execution risk and insurer participation rates are unknowns that traders should monitor daily. On the demand side, the record U.S. electricity generation of 4.43 TWh in 2025 underscores a structural consumption floor that limits downside even in softer macro environments, though the weak jobs report flagged this week introduces a demand-destruction risk if consumer sentiment deteriorates sharply on $4-plus gasoline. Key near-term catalysts to watch: any Trump-Iran diplomatic signal, IEA SPR decision reversal, tanker insurance program uptake data, and whether Brent can sustain a close above $95 — a move there would likely force a fundamental reassessment of upside targets toward $105-$110.
