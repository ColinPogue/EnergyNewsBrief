Top Stories
Strait of Hormuz Near-Shutdown Sends Brent Above $90
Rigzone / Financial Times / CNBC / Upstream Online
Maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has ground to a near-complete halt, with no oil shipments transiting in the past 24 hours as the U.S.-Israeli Operation Epic Fury against Iran enters its seventh day. Brent crude surged above $90 per barrel for the first time since the conflict began, with traders bracing for a prolonged disruption and further production shutdowns across the Gulf. Energy exporters are scrambling to identify alternative routes as a growing backlog of tankers trapped in the Persian Gulf threatens to deepen the global supply crunch.
Trump Demands Iran Surrender; No Deal Without Capitulation
CNBC / OilPrice
President Trump declared there will be no diplomatic resolution to the Iran war short of unconditional surrender, effectively closing the door on near-term ceasefire negotiations and sustaining the geopolitical risk premium in oil markets. Brent futures broke through $90 per barrel following Trump's statement, while the national average gasoline price posted its largest single-week jump since the early days of the Russia-Ukraine war. A deteriorating jobs report is adding pressure on the administration, with economists warning that sustained high fuel prices could drag on consumer sentiment and broader economic growth.
Qatar Signals Indefinite Ras Laffan LNG Shutdown Until War Ends
NGI / Financial Times
QatarEnergy CEO Saad Sherida Al-Kaabi told the Financial Times that the Ras Laffan Industrial Complex will not restart LNG production until the Middle East conflict concludes entirely, signaling an open-ended outage at one of the world's largest LNG export hubs. With production offline, QatarEnergy has begun leasing idle LNG tankers, a move that could tighten the global LNG shipping market and lift freight rates. U.S. natural gas futures rallied in sympathy with oil as the market priced in the prospect of a prolonged gap in Qatari LNG supply.
IEA Holds Off on Emergency Oil Reserve Release Despite Crisis
World Oil
The International Energy Agency said it sees no immediate need to authorize a release of member nations' strategic petroleum reserves despite the escalating Iran conflict and the Strait of Hormuz disruption. The decision suggests the IEA believes current stockpiles and alternative supply routes provide sufficient near-term buffer, though analysts warn that assessment could change rapidly if the Hormuz closure extends beyond days. The IEA's posture places greater pressure on OPEC spare capacity holders and U.S. producers to compensate for lost Gulf flows.
Reliance Returns to Russian Crude After U.S. Grants India Waiver
OilPrice
India's Reliance Industries is actively sounding the market for Russian crude purchases after the United States issued a temporary one-month license allowing India to buy Russian-origin oil loaded on or before March 5, providing a narrow but significant sanctions carve-out. The waiver marks a notable policy pivot as Washington balances its Russia sanctions regime against the need to keep Indian refiners supplied amid a tightening global crude market driven by the Iran conflict. Reliance, one of the world's largest private refiners, had previously stepped back from Russian barrels under sanctions pressure, making its return a meaningful signal for Urals price dynamics and Asian crude flows.
Market Outlook
Today's price action is being driven by a rare confluence of simultaneous supply shocks — Hormuz near-shutdown, indefinite Ras Laffan LNG outage, and a U.S. president publicly ruling out ceasefire negotiations — that has compressed months of geopolitical risk premium into a single session. With Brent at $92.68 and WTI at $90.70, the market is now pricing in a sustained disruption, and the IEA's decision to withhold SPR authorization removes a key downside hedge for bulls, placing the supply-response burden squarely on U.S. shale producers and OPEC spare capacity — primarily Saudi Arabia and the UAE — who must now decide whether to open the taps. The WTI futures curve's steep contango — $90.50 in Apr 2026 collapsing to $74.52 by Aug 2026 — suggests the market views the disruption as acute but ultimately finite, meaning any credible ceasefire signal or Hormuz reopening could trigger a violent reversal of 8-12% within hours; traders holding long positions must size risk accordingly. On the demand side, the macroeconomic feedback loop is increasingly dangerous: national gasoline prices are posting their largest weekly jump since the Russia-Ukraine war's early days, a deteriorating jobs report is already pressuring the administration, and sustained fuel price shock at this magnitude historically begins eroding consumer demand within 6-8 weeks. The India-Russia crude waiver issued by Washington is a notable policy tell — it signals the U.S. is quietly managing tightness behind the scenes even as Trump publicly escalates, and Reliance's return to Urals barrels will provide some relief to Asian refinery margins while nudging Urals prices off their sanctions-era discounts. Key catalysts to watch in the next 48-72 hours: any change in Hormuz transit status, a Saudi Aramco production increase announcement, IEA emergency meeting signals, and whether Trump's rhetoric shifts even marginally toward negotiation — any one of these could define the next $10 move in either direction.
