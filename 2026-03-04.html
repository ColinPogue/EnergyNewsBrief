Top Stories
Hormuz Crisis Cripples Gulf Output, QatarEnergy Declares Force Majeure
OilPrice / World Oil / Upstream Online / CNBC / NGI
The Strait of Hormuz remains paralyzed as Iranian drone strikes have forced Iraq to begin shutting production at the Rumaila supergiant field and idled major Gulf refineries, triggering a severe storage crunch as trapped Middle East crude has nowhere to go. QatarEnergy has declared LNG force majeure, threatening to create an 'LNG vacuum in the Atlantic Basin' that has European buyers scrambling for alternatives. U.S. crude surged before easing after Treasury Secretary Bessent pledged naval escorts and tanker insurance, while natural gas futures pulled back mid-week as traders awaited ceasefire signals.
Ras Tanura Attacked Again, Gulf Safe-Haven Status Erodes
Rigzone / Financial Times
Saudi Arabia confirmed an attempted attack on Ras Tanura, its largest oil refinery and one of the world's most critical energy infrastructure nodes, marking another strike on Gulf energy assets amid the broader Iran conflict. The Financial Times notes that Iran's retaliatory campaign is systematically dismantling the Gulf region's long-held reputation as a zone of geopolitical stability. The back-to-back attacks on key Saudi facilities are raising significant risk premiums for traders exposed to Middle East crude flows.
Germany's SEFE Locks In Argentine LNG in Historic Eight-Year Deal
NGI
Germany's SEFE signed a landmark agreement to purchase up to 2 million tons per year of LNG from Argentina's Southern Energy SA over eight years, marking Argentina's first-ever long-term LNG export contract. The deal reflects Europe's intensifying drive to diversify away from vulnerable supply corridors as the Iran conflict disrupts Qatari and Gulf LNG flows. For traders, it signals growing Atlantic Basin LNG supply buildout and a structural shift in European procurement strategy.
Nuclear reactor restart in Japan will likely displace natural gas electricity generation
EIA
On February 9, 2026, Japan restarted Unit 6 of its largest nuclear power plant, the Kashiwazaki-Kariwa Nuclear Power Station in Niigata Prefecture, which was shut down following the 2011 Fukushima tsunami and nuclear accident.
Trump has an AI data center problem ahead of the midterms — with no easy solutions
CNBC
Grassroots opposition to data centers is growing in communities across the U.S. as people blame the facilities for high utility bills.
Market Outlook
Energy markets are trading in a bifurcated regime: crude is grinding higher on hard geopolitical supply risk while natural gas is pricing in demand erosion and supply diversification. The Hormuz paralysis is not a tail risk anymore — it is the base case, with Rumaila curtailments and idled Gulf refineries creating a physical supply crunch that could push Brent toward $84-85 if no ceasefire materializes within the next 7-10 trading sessions. The Ras Tanura attack confirmation is the more underappreciated risk; a successful strike on that facility would remove a critical refining node from the global system and trigger a spike well beyond current levels, making $90 Brent a realistic stress scenario. On the natural gas side, the 4.62% drop to $2.91 reflects a market that is actively repricing the demand side: Japan's Kashiwazaki-Kariwa Unit 6 restart is a structural negative for LNG pull, while the SEFE-Argentina eight-year deal signals that Atlantic Basin buyers are building supply resilience that reduces panic-buying pressure even amid QatarEnergy's force majeure. The key macro headwind remains the AI data center buildout uncertainty — grassroots opposition and utility bill backlash could slow the US power demand growth story that bulls have been counting on to absorb excess gas supply. Traders should watch WTI $76.00 resistance and NG $2.80 support as the binary inflection points: a WTI break higher confirms the geopolitical premium is structural, while an NG break below $2.80 signals the demand destruction and nuclear displacement thesis is winning out over the LNG supply shock narrative.
