QatarEnergy Force Majeure Triggers Global LNG Market Shock
Upstream Online / OilPrice / Financial Times
QatarEnergy has declared force majeure on LNG deliveries as the Iran war effectively closes the Strait of Hormuz to Qatari cargoes, jolting global gas markets that were already tight heading into spring. Europe, which had been attracting the bulk of LNG spot cargoes amid depleted inventories, now faces the prospect of an 'LNG vacuum in the Atlantic Basin' as Qatari supply drops out of the market. Coal prices have simultaneously surged as utilities scramble for alternative fuels, raising the prospect of restarting mothballed coal plants.
Germany's SEFE Locks In Argentine LNG in Landmark 8-Year Deal
NGI
Germany's Securing Energy for Europe (SEFE) has signed an eight-year deal to purchase up to 2 million tons per year of LNG from Southern Energy SA in Argentina, marking Argentina's first-ever long-term LNG supply contract. The agreement represents a significant step in Europe's drive to diversify away from Russian and Middle Eastern gas supplies, and gains added urgency as the Iran war disrupts Qatari LNG flows. The deal signals growing investor confidence in Argentina's emerging LNG export industry under its new energy liberalization framework.
Japan Nuclear Restart Set to Displace LNG Demand at Critical Moment
EIA
Japan restarted Unit 6 of the Kashiwazaki-Kariwa Nuclear Power Station on February 9, 2026 — the country's largest nuclear plant — which had been offline since the 2011 Fukushima disaster. The return to full operations is expected to displace a meaningful volume of natural gas-fired generation in Japan, one of the world's top LNG importers, reducing spot demand at a time when global LNG supply is already under severe pressure from the Iran conflict. Traders should monitor whether reduced Japanese LNG pull offers any partial offset to the Atlantic Basin supply squeeze driven by Qatari force majeure.

Market Outlook
The market is pricing a genuine supply emergency, not a sentiment trade — Iran's campaign has effectively weaponized the Strait of Hormuz, and the cascading effects on Saudi output capacity, Qatari LNG flows, and global freight markets represent a multi-commodity shock of the first order. WTI at $77.28 and Brent at $83.84 are moving targets if Ras Tanura sustains meaningful damage or Saudi Arabia is forced into output cuts within the two-week window analysts are flagging; either scenario would invalidate Standard Chartered's freshly hiked $74/barrel forecast almost immediately. On natural gas, QatarEnergy's force majeure is the single most consequential near-term catalyst — Europe was already running tight heading into spring, and an LNG vacuum in the Atlantic Basin could push TTF and US Henry Hub materially higher, with $3.00 on NG acting as the first line of resistance before a run toward $3.20 becomes plausible. The Japan nuclear restart at Kashiwazaki-Kariwa is the one bearish offset worth monitoring: meaningfully reduced Japanese LNG spot demand could free up Pacific Basin cargoes for rerouting, but the logistics of redirecting supply from Pacific to Atlantic markets are slow and the relief would likely arrive too late for near-term European storage. Macro risk is not trivial — US military casualties in Kuwait and the near-miss at Al Udeid raise the probability of direct US escalation, which would amplify the supply disruption but also risk demand destruction via a broader economic shock. Key watch items: daily Hormuz shipping traffic data, any Saudi Aramco operational update on Ras Tanura, the next EIA crude and gas storage prints, and whether US naval escort commitments translate into actual tanker movement — any failure there is the fastest route to a $90 Brent print.
