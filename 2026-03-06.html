Top Stories
Brent Tops $90 as Hormuz Near-Shutdown Halts Gulf Oil Flows
Rigzone / OilPrice / Financial Times / World Oil / Upstream Online
The Strait of Hormuz has ground to a near-complete halt with no oil shipments transiting in the past 24 hours, sending Brent crude above $90 per barrel for the first time since the U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran began. Iraq and Kuwait are cutting output, Qatar has warned that Gulf energy exports could stop entirely within days and that it could take weeks to months to restore deliveries, while a growing backlog of tankers in the Persian Gulf threatens to worsen supply disruptions the longer the closure persists. The IEA has so far signaled no need to release emergency reserves, leaving markets to price in an increasingly open-ended conflict.
Trump Demands Iran's Unconditional Surrender, Spurning Deal
CNBC
President Trump declared he will accept no deal to end the Iran war short of unconditional surrender, a statement that immediately drove Brent futures above $90 per barrel as traders priced in a prolonged conflict with no near-term diplomatic off-ramp. Trump has also proposed U.S. Navy escorts for tankers through the Gulf, though analysts warn the logistics and rules of engagement make the plan extremely difficult to execute at the scale needed to meaningfully offset the Hormuz closure. The dual signals — maximum pressure rhetoric combined with an unworkable maritime workaround — are reinforcing market expectations of sustained supply disruption.
U.S. Gasoline Hits Record Under Trump as Iran War Bites
OilPrice / CNBC
The national average gasoline price has surged to its highest level of either Trump term, driven by the de facto closure of the Strait of Hormuz and its ripple effect on global crude and refined product supply, according to GasBuddy data. WTI crude jumped from roughly $67 per barrel on February 27, just before hostilities began, to well above $80 in the days that followed, with further upside risk as the conflict drags on. A weak Friday jobs report is now adding a stagflationary dimension to the crisis, testing the administration's willingness to absorb economic pain in pursuit of its Iran policy.
Natural Gas Futures Rally Hard as Iran War Reverberates Through LNG Markets
NGI / EIA
U.S. natural gas futures are on track for strong weekly gains as U.S.-Israeli strikes on Iran disrupt global LNG supply chains and elevate energy risk premiums across the board. The conflict-driven rally is overshadowing regional bearish signals, including a narrowing of the PG&E Citygate-Sumas spread to a one-year low on slumping Pacific demand. Traders are also digesting EIA data showing U.S. LNG developers signed 40 million tons per annum — roughly 5.2 Bcf/d — in new sale and purchase agreements in 2025, the highest volume since 2022, underscoring long-term confidence in U.S. export capacity even amid near-term supply chaos.
U.S. electricity generation in 2025 hit a record, again
EIA
U.S. electricity net generation reached a record in 2025 based on data from our Electricity Data Browser. In 2025, the United States generated 4.43 terawatthours (TWh) of electricity, up 2.8% from 2024 generation, which previously had been the highest annual total in our Monthly Energy Review Data Browser dataset dating back to 1949.
Market Outlook
The energy complex is now in full geopolitical crisis mode, with WTI at $91.44 and Brent at $93.54 reflecting a market that is pricing in a sustained, open-ended Hormuz closure rather than a swift resolution. Trump's unconditional surrender demand has effectively closed the diplomatic exit, and with Iraq, Kuwait, and Qatar all signaling output and export disruptions, the supply shock is broadening beyond Iran itself — the IEA's refusal so far to authorize strategic reserve releases is leaving an already tight physical market entirely exposed to conflict risk. The steep WTI backwardation — Apr 2026 at $91.31 versus Aug 2026 at $74.20, a $17 spread across just five months — tells traders the market sees this as a near-term crisis rather than a permanent supply restructuring, but that calculus shifts materially if the Hormuz closure extends beyond weeks into months, as Qatar has warned. The stagflationary dimension is the key macro wildcard: the weak Friday jobs report arriving simultaneously with record U.S. gasoline prices creates a toxic policy bind for the administration, limiting its appetite to absorb prolonged economic pain and potentially accelerating pressure for a negotiated off-ramp — or alternatively, hardening the military posture if domestic politics demand action. On the natural gas side, the $3.20 print is war-premium driven and fundamentally fragile; Pacific demand weakness and the Citygate-Sumas spread compression remind traders that a ceasefire or Hormuz reopening could see a sharp giveback, even as the record 40 MTPA in new U.S. LNG SPAs signed in 2025 anchors longer-term bullish conviction. Key catalysts to watch: any IEA emergency reserve announcement, U.S. Navy tanker escort operationalization, Qatar's 72-hour export window, and whether next week's jobless claims and CPI data force a Fed or White House policy response to the stagflation threat.
