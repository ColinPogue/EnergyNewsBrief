Top Stories
Strait of Hormuz Near-Shutdown Sends Brent Above $90
Rigzone / OilPrice / Financial Times / Upstream Online
Maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has ground to a near-complete halt, with no oil shipments recorded in the past 24 hours as the US-Iran conflict enters its seventh day. Brent crude broke above $90 per barrel for the first time since the war began, while Iraq and Kuwait have begun cutting output and Qatar has warned Gulf energy exports could stop entirely 'within days,' with weeks to months needed to restore deliveries. Energy exporters are scrambling to find alternative routes out of the Gulf as traders brace for a prolonged conflict and further production shutdowns.
Trump Demands Iran 'Unconditional Surrender,' Roiling Markets
CNBC / Financial Times
President Trump declared there will be no deal with Iran short of unconditional surrender, a statement that immediately pushed Brent crude higher through the $90 per barrel threshold and rattled broader financial markets. The hardline stance reduces near-term prospects for a ceasefire, signaling a potentially extended disruption to Gulf energy flows. A simultaneous weak US jobs report has raised questions about the economic durability of the conflict's energy price shock.
US Gasoline Hits Trump-Era High; Navy Tanker Escort Plan Questioned
OilPrice / CNBC
US retail gasoline prices have surged to their highest level during either of President Trump's terms, driven by the de facto closure of the Strait of Hormuz and the resulting pullback in crude and refined product flows. WTI jumped sharply from $67 per barrel on February 27 to well above $80 as Hormuz disruptions deepened, with California identified as particularly exposed given its geographic isolation from domestic pipeline infrastructure. Trump's proposal to use the US Navy to escort tankers through the Gulf faces significant logistical and security obstacles that analysts say could limit its effectiveness.
Natural Gas Futures Rally Hard as Iran War Disrupts Global LNG
NGI
US natural gas futures are on track for significant weekly gains after US-Israeli strikes on Iran disrupted global LNG supply chains, tightening the market and lifting both near-term and forward prices. Forward curves for summer 2026 and winter 2026-27 have moved higher as traders weigh the potential for sustained LNG demand increases to draw down domestic US supplies. The conflict has reinforced the strategic importance of US LNG export capacity as Gulf-sourced supply becomes increasingly unreliable.
IEA Holds Off on Emergency Oil Reserve Release Despite Iran Crisis
World Oil
The International Energy Agency has signaled it does not yet see sufficient grounds to authorize a coordinated release of emergency strategic petroleum reserves, even as the Iran conflict drives Brent above $90 per barrel and Hormuz flows collapse. The decision leaves markets without a key potential buffer at a critical moment, with supply disruptions already measurable in tanker traffic data. Traders will be watching IEA communications closely for any shift in posture if the conflict and price rally continue to escalate.
Market Outlook
The near-complete halt of Strait of Hormuz tanker traffic is the most significant supply disruption since the 1973 Arab oil embargo, and markets are only beginning to price the full consequences — Brent at $92.57 and WTI at $90.20 represent a floor, not a ceiling, if the conflict extends beyond the current seven-day window. Trump's demand for Iranian unconditional surrender eliminates any near-term diplomatic off-ramp, and with Iraq, Kuwait, and Qatar signaling further output reductions, the physical supply deficit could widen materially in coming sessions; the steep WTI backwardation curve — spot at $90.32 versus Aug 2026 at $74.08 — confirms the market views this as acute rather than structural, but that calculus changes fast if Hormuz remains closed for weeks. The IEA's decision to hold off on emergency SPR releases is the single biggest wildcard: any coordinated release announcement would be the sharpest near-term bearish catalyst available, capable of driving a $5-8 intraday reversal, and traders should keep tight stops on long positions around IEA communication windows. On the demand side, the simultaneous weak US jobs report injects a macro headwind that could cap rallies if recession fears accelerate — energy demand destruction at the consumer level is already visible in the gasoline price surge to Trump-era highs, which historically acts as its own demand suppressant within 4-6 weeks. Natural gas at $3.18 deserves close attention as a secondary trade: US LNG export infrastructure is being repriced as critical geopolitical infrastructure, and any further Gulf LNG disruption or new long-term offtake announcements could push Henry Hub toward $3.50 before summer injection season. Key risks to the long thesis: a surprise ceasefire or backchannel negotiations, an unilateral US SPR release, or a sharper-than-expected US economic slowdown that forces demand forecasts lower — any one of these could trigger a $6-10 correction in WTI within 48 hours.
