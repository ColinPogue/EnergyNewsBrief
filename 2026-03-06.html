Top Stories
Strait of Hormuz Near Shutdown as Brent Breaks $90
Rigzone / Financial Times / Upstream Online / CNBC
Maritime traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has ground to a near-complete halt, with no oil shipments recorded in the past 24 hours amid the widening U.S.-Israeli Operation Epic Fury against Iran. Brent crude surged above $90 per barrel for the first time since the conflict began, as traders priced in a prolonged disruption and further production shutdowns across the Gulf region. Energy exporters are scrambling to identify alternative routing options as fears of a sustained global supply crunch intensify.
Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG Shutdown Indefinite Until Conflict Ends
NGI
QatarEnergy CEO Saad Sherida Al-Kaabi told the Financial Times that the Ras Laffan Industrial Complex — a cornerstone of global LNG supply — will not restart production until the Middle East conflict is fully resolved, with no timeline given. Qatar has begun leasing idle LNG tankers as the facility sits shuttered, raising urgent supply concerns for buyers across Europe and Asia. The shutdown adds a significant LNG dimension to a crisis already threatening crude flows through the Strait of Hormuz.
Trump Demands Unconditional Surrender; Launches $20B Tanker Insurance Program
CNBC
President Trump declared he will accept no deal with Iran short of unconditional surrender, eliminating near-term prospects for a negotiated ceasefire and sustaining upward pressure on oil prices. In parallel, the Trump administration announced a $20 billion government reinsurance program for oil tankers operating in the region, an emergency measure aimed at keeping whatever residual shipping remains commercially viable as the war enters its second week. The dual announcements signal Washington is preparing for a prolonged conflict with lasting energy market consequences.
IEA Holds Back Reserve Release; Iraqi Kurdistan Oilfield Attacked
World Oil / Upstream Online
The International Energy Agency said it sees no immediate need to release emergency strategic petroleum reserves despite the accelerating Iran crisis, a stance likely to disappoint markets looking for official intervention to cap the price spike. Meanwhile, an oilfield in Iraqi Kurdistan was attacked just three days after operations there had already been suspended, further eroding supply from a key non-Gulf producing region. The combination of IEA inaction and fresh infrastructure strikes reinforces a bearish supply outlook for traders.
Reliance Gets US Waiver to Resume Russian Crude Purchases
OilPrice
India's Reliance Industries is actively sounding the market for Russian crude after the U.S. Treasury issued a temporary one-month license allowing India to purchase Russian-origin oil loaded on or before March 5, according to sources cited by Bloomberg. The waiver marks a notable policy shift that reopens a major demand channel for Russian barrels at a moment when Middle East supply disruptions are already tightening global markets. Traders will watch whether additional country-specific waivers follow, potentially reshaping near-term crude flow patterns.
Market Outlook
The market is in full-scale war-premium repricing mode, and today's moves — WTI +12.38%, Brent +8.58%, NG +5.46% — reflect not just spot tightness but a fundamental reassessment of Middle East supply risk duration. The Strait of Hormuz near-shutdown is the single most disruptive supply event since the 1973 embargo; with no tanker traffic recorded in 24 hours and Trump demanding unconditional Iranian surrender, there is no credible near-term resolution scenario traders can model for a reversal. The IEA's decision to hold SPR releases is a critical bearish wildcard that failed to materialize — its absence removes the most reliable institutional price cap, and markets have taken note aggressively. Compounding the crude shock, Qatar's Ras Laffan shutdown introduces a major LNG dimension that could push European gas storage draws into emergency territory by summer if the conflict extends beyond 4–6 weeks, with Asian LNG spot prices likely to surge in sympathy. On the demand side, the Reliance-Russia waiver opens a modest relief valve — Russian barrels re-entering Indian refineries could redirect some non-Middle East supply, but the volume is insufficient to offset Hormuz-scale disruption and the one-month license creates its own uncertainty cliff. Key watchpoints for tomorrow: any Hormuz tanker movement, follow-on SPR decisions from IEA member states, and whether the Iraqi Kurdistan attack expands into a broader non-OPEC supply disruption story that adds another leg to the rally.
